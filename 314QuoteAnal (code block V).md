# IV. NOTHINGNESS AS A TEMPORAL EXPRESSION OF THE TRANSCENDENT

### 1\. The Paradox of Divine Absence

In many mystical writings, **God** is discovered precisely where God seems most absent. This is the “negative theology” approach, where language fails, illusions collapse, and only an ineffable emptiness remains. “God” can thus be hypothesized as not a _being_ among others, but the **transcendent dimension** manifested in the very experience of no ground.

**Nihiltheistic Assertion**: The “temporal expression” of the Transcendent might be “the experience of utter meaninglessness,” because that is the moment illusions are shed, unveiling a deeper resonance that defies explanation.

### 2\. Time-Bound Emptiness, Eternal Echoes

**Time** intensifies the sting of emptiness: we are ephemeral. **Eternity** becomes either a longing or a terror. Yet in the _dark night_ of ephemeral illusions, there is a fleeting sense that “something” else might stir in the shadows. The tension of time vs. timelessness, ephemeral vs. infinite, spawns the religious sense that **Nothingness** is not final but paradoxically “full.”

### 3\. Lived Religious Experience of Nihilism

Consider:

- **Cioran’s faithfulness to emptiness** as an odd kind of unwavering devotion to the ultimate nothing.
- **Tillich’s “Ground of Being”** that appears only when God “disappears.”

**Nihiltheism** merges these threads: the “religious” aspect is that the experience of losing all illusions might ironically reveal or echo something we can call “Transcendent” (not a personal deity, but an emergent dimension glimpsed in radical negation).

# V. CROSS-CULTURAL, MULTI-RELIGIOUS STRENGTHENING OF NIHILTHEISM

### 1\. Taoist Non-Being

**Taoism** introduces _wu_, “non-being,” as the source from which all being flows. This resonates with Nihiltheism’s claim that **Nothingness** is the groundless ground, generative of possibility rather than pure void.

### 2\. Kabbalistic Ayin

In **Jewish mysticism**, _Ayin_ (nothingness) stands at the apex of divinity, prior to manifestation. The infinite (Ein Sof) emerges from the formless _Ayin_. This again parallels Nihiltheism’s stance that from the “no-thing,” a dimension of the “everything” can arise.

### 3\. Sufi Fana

Sufi mystics speak of _fana_, the annihilation of the self in God’s presence. This notion deeply aligns with Eckhart’s and Molinos’ emphasis on total surrender, and with Buddhist dissolution of the ego. The negative or emptiness-laden approach becomes the route to the mystical Real.

**Conclusion**: Whether _fana_, _ayin_, _wu_, or _śūnyatā_, we see an abiding global motif that the **negation** of illusory structures can birth an “experience of the Transcendent,” bridging cultures and epochs.
